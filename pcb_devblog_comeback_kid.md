# The Comeback Kid

## How a childhood dream died, came back, and became Pixel City Bros

---

Growing up, I lived two lives. On the field, I was an athlete — basketball, football, whatever was in season. At home, I was a closet nerd glued to a Super Nintendo.

The game that changed everything was Final Fantasy III. I know it's technically Final Fantasy VI, but in the US it was marketed as III, and that's how I'll always remember it. The story, the characters, the turn-based combat — it rewired my brain. I didn't just want to play games. I wanted to *make* them.

But FF3 wasn't my only obsession. I loved the weird, scrappy sports games too — River City Ransom, Super Dodge Ball, Crash and the Boys Street Challenge. Something about those chunky pixel art characters beating each other up in the streets just hit different. The art style stuck with me for decades.

I started programming in 7th grade. This was the 90s — no YouTube tutorials, no Stack Overflow. Just curiosity and a lot of bad code. Making a game went on the bucket list. It stayed there for a long time.

---

## The First Attempt

Fast forward to my 30s. I'm a full-time software developer with a family, and the indie game boom is happening. Games like Cthulhu Saves the World and River City Ransom Underground proved that small teams could ship real games with retro aesthetics. The dream woke back up.

I saw Dungeon Boss — this mobile game that was blowing up at the time — and had a thought: *What if this was a retro pixel art game inspired by River City Ransom?* A team-based brawler RPG with that classic sprite style.

I pitched the idea on a game jam site. Got a group of guys together. We were going to build something incredible.

Like a summer band, it fell apart in weeks. Turns out, building a multiplayer game that rivals established studios is a terrible first project. The idea was dead before it started. But I met some good people along the way.

---

## The Money Pit

So I pivoted. Forget multiplayer — just build a single-player turn-based RPG. My brother and I decided to go all in. We hired designers, programmers, freelancers. We threw money at it.

And we got far. We had a full world built out. Overworld maps, dungeons with random encounters, character sprites, environments — the works. We had enough assets to build a complete game.

But I couldn't nail the battle system. It started as a Legends of Dragoon-inspired timed-hit system. Then I changed it. Then I changed it again. The scope kept creeping. I wasn't managing the project well — changing my mind, second-guessing decisions, burning through contractors.

The money added up. The passion turned to stress. The thing I loved most became the thing I dreaded opening. I shelved it.

---

## The Dark Ages

For years, the project sat there. I'd think about it sometimes. I still had all the assets, all the design docs, all the characters living in my head. But I had a family, a career, and no time to write game code myself.

Here's the thing that killed me: I'm a web developer. JavaScript, PHP, the whole stack. Theoretically, you *can* build a game with web tech — Electron, progressive web apps, Phaser. I tried. It never felt right. You can't easily port a web game to consoles. The tooling isn't there. It always felt like I was forcing a square peg into a round hole.

So the dream went dormant. Not dead — dormant.

---

## The Epiphany

Then AI happened.

When Claude Opus dropped, I tried using it for game development in Unity. The code was solid, but Unity's visual editor was the bottleneck. AI can write brilliant code, but it struggles when it has to interact with a complex GUI. There's an MCP plugin now, but it's not there yet.

That's when it clicked: *AI writes best when there's no user interface in the way. Pure code. Pure logic.*

I started looking at game frameworks that are 100% code — no visual editor, no drag-and-drop, no scene trees. Just you and the compiler. Godot was an option. MonoGame was the other.

I gave MonoGame a shot with Claude, and I was completely taken aback. It was *brilliant*. With my guidance and experience directing the code, we were building real game systems in hours instead of weeks. The hub screen, the shop, the menu systems — things that would have taken me months were coming together in days.

I knew from that moment: the dream is alive again.

---

## The Game That Emerged

But here's the thing — I didn't just resurrect the old project. I rebuilt the vision from scratch.

The original game was a traditional RPG. Walk around an overworld. Enter dungeons. Random encounters. Walk to the shop. Walk back. Walk to the next town. It's a formula that works, but it's also a formula that demands hundreds of hours of content to feel complete.

I thought about what I actually loved about mobile games like Dungeon Boss. The hub. The quick access to everything. Train your guys, buy gear, jump into a fight — all from one screen. No wasted time. The problem with those games? Pay-to-win mechanics, zero story, and an endless PvP treadmill.

So I merged the two ideas.

**Pixel City Bros is a single-player RPG with the soul of Final Fantasy and the efficiency of a mobile game.**

No overworld walking. No running in circles to trigger random encounters. No in-app purchases. You buy it once for a few bucks on the app store, and you get a complete 5-10 hour RPG experience. Board a flight in New York, and you'll have it nearly finished by the time you land in LA. Finish it on the trip back.

---

## How It Works

The entire game runs through a hub — a pixel art city skyline where every building is a system:

- **Story Mode** takes you through 5 acts across Pixel City. Each act is a section of the city with its own vibe — bikers, brainwashed high schoolers, rogue AI, a Yakuza tower. Some stages play full cinematic cutscenes like you'd expect from Final Fantasy. Others are quick dialogue beats to keep the story moving. Every chapter ends with a boss battle.

- **The Gym** is a mini idle-game where you train your fighters and raise a protege through a 12-fight ladder. This is where your power comes from — not buying better swords at a shop.

- **The Pit** is an underground fight circuit for grinding. Think *The Warriors* — fight through 5 gang territories, cash out when you want, or push your luck for bigger rewards.

- **The Shop** sells consumables and accessories. That's it. Your strength comes from training, not shopping.

Each chapter also brings a guest ally to your team — a washed-up wrestler, a high school quarterback, a hacker, a sensei — each one changing how you approach combat. The battle system is Octopath Traveler meets MMA: four strike types, a break system, Bro Sync attacks, and a mode called "On Fire" when you max out your Bro Power.

Two bros. Five acts. One city. No filler.

---

## Where We Are Now

The hub screen is built. The shop system is complete. The backpack and menu systems are implemented. The game design is fully documented — every system, every boss, every piece of dialogue.

The battle system is next.

I have almost all the design assets from the original project. The story is written. The characters are alive. And for the first time in years, I'm not stressed about this project — I'm excited.
